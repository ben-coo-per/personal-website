There are 265,000 people with a spinal cord injury in the United States alone. Losing the ability to stand comes with social and health issues, in addition to the more practical challenges. Nearly everyone we spoke to with paraplegia mentioned they felt as though they had lost a significant amount of their independence.

![Gallery image 1](/cms-assets/projects/solo-stander/gallery-1.png)

To begin this project, as a team we spoke with over 40 people consisting of people with paraplegia and quadriplegia, their families, physical therapists, and spinal injury specialists, the majority of our interviewees with paraplegia told us that their core motivation for regaining the ability to stand upright was to regain some independence. They wanted to do things like standing up with the crowd at a baseball game, putting dishes away in a high-up cabinet, or helping prep a meal at the kitchen counter.

![Gallery image 2](/cms-assets/projects/solo-stander/gallery-2.png)

With regards to the actual device, our interviews with specialists and potential users taught us that we must design a device that would be lightweight to avoid shoulder strain, low cost to circumvent the insurance process that only pays chairs & accessories every 5 years, and an aftermarket attachment so users could keep their existing, custom-fitted chair
