Breathing is an overlooked performance tool in running.  Unlike pace, cadence, and form, breath, the body’s most immediate signal of strain or sync, is often ignored. But training your breath awareness sharpens proprioception and interoception—helping runners sense fatigue earlier, move more efficiently, and reduce injury risk. It also helps bring runners into flow state, in which runners move with more consistency, precision, and ease.

My team spoke with over 20 runners, endurance athletes, physical therapists, and doctors over the course of 4 weeks to find out why breath training is so underutilized at the hobbyist running level. 

![Gallery image 1](/cms-assets/projects/nose-thing/gallery-1.png)

We found that: 
1. Breathing is really easy to forget mid-run, especially when you’re tired 
1. Current activity tracking devices typically focus on post-run analysis of your data, not real-time feedback. This makes the feedback-correction loop take days, not minutes. The devices that do provide realtime feedback rely on distilled alert messages, but by just amplifying the natural signal of our breath’s sound, we can alert the same message while training body awareness.
1. Many people don’t want to give up listening to their music/podcasts/audiobooks while running to focus on their breath.

![Gallery image 2](/cms-assets/projects/nose-thing/gallery-2.png)

This led us to create Nose Thing, a concept for a connected nasal dilator that makes your breath audible over your music. This initially sounds really annoying, but through user testing we found that varying the audio-mix of the breath and music depending on the breath patterns & rhythm can bring awareness when needed and fade to the background when not.

![Gallery image 3](/cms-assets/projects/nose-thing/gallery-3.png)

Our prototyping took place over the course of two weeks. During this time we brainstormed over 40 ideas and trialed over 10. This was one set of prototypes and tests that ended up progressing forward to inform our final concept.
